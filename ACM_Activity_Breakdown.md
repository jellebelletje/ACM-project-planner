### 1. Assess Sponsorship in a Structured Interview

#### To-dos
- [ ] Identify the executive sponsor and any co-sponsors for this initiative
- [ ] Schedule a 60–90 minute structured interview with each sponsor
- [ ] Prepare the interview question set (see Questions below)
- [ ] Send pre-read materials to the sponsor 48 hours before the interview (project brief, scope summary, expected role of sponsor)
- [ ] Conduct the interview and document findings in a sponsor readiness matrix (using Prosci's CLARC model: Coalition building, Leadership, Active & visible participation, Resistance management, Communication)
- [ ] Score sponsor readiness across each CLARC dimension and identify gaps
- [ ] Develop a sponsor coaching plan to address identified gaps
- [ ] _[Project-specific: Add any additional sponsor-related tasks based on organisational structure, e.g., if there are multiple business unit sponsors who need aligning]_

#### Questions

**Vision and Commitment:**
- What does success look like to you personally for this initiative? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_
- Why is this change important now? What happens if we don't do it? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_
- How does this initiative connect to the broader organisational strategy? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_
- What are you personally willing to do visibly to support this change? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_

**Resources and Priority:**
- What resources (budget, people, time) are you prepared to allocate? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_
- What will you defend when competing priorities emerge? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_
- Where does this rank among your current priorities? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_

**Risk Awareness:**
- What do you see as the biggest risks to adoption? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_
- Which groups do you expect to resist, and why? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_
- How have you handled change resistance in previous initiatives? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_

**Coalition:**
- Who else in the leadership team actively supports this? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_
- Are there leaders who are sceptical or opposed? Who? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_
- Who do people in the organisation actually listen to, regardless of title? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_

**Communication:**
- How do you currently communicate major changes to the organisation? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_
- Are you willing to send communications, attend town halls, and record video messages? → _Ask: Executive Sponsor_

#### Answers
_(To be filled in per project)_

#### Particularisation Guidance
For **technology rollouts** (e.g., M365 Copilot), probe specifically whether the sponsor has personal experience with the technology and whether they will use it visibly. For **process changes**, focus on whether the sponsor understands operational impact at the frontline level. For **restructuring or mergers**, assess the sponsor's willingness to have difficult conversations about role changes, and whether they have the authority to make decisions about affected populations. Add questions about budget authority if the initiative requires significant investment.

### 2. Stakeholder Identification and Influence Mapping

#### To-dos
- [ ] Obtain the organisational chart for all affected business units
- [ ] Conduct a preliminary stakeholder brainstorm with the sponsor and project manager to identify all individuals and groups who are affected by, or can influence, the change
- [ ] Categorise stakeholders by level: senior leaders, middle managers, frontline staff, support functions (IT, HR, L&D), external parties
- [ ] Schedule structured interviews with senior leaders and their direct reports (30–45 minutes each)
- [ ] Prepare the interview question set (see Questions below)
- [ ] Conduct interviews and document each stakeholder's influence level and disposition (supportive, neutral, resistant)
- [ ] Create a stakeholder map with axes of influence (high/low) and disposition (supportive/neutral/resistant)
- [ ] Identify priority stakeholders: high-influence resistors (need converting) and high-influence supporters (need equipping)
- [ ] _[Project-specific: Add stakeholders unique to this project, e.g., union representatives, regulatory bodies, vendor partners]_

#### Questions

**Role and Scope:**
- What is your role, and how does your team interact with the area being changed? → _Ask: Each identified stakeholder_
- How many people report to you directly and indirectly? → _Ask: Each identified stakeholder_
- Who else should I be speaking to about this change? → _Ask: Each identified stakeholder_

**Disposition:**
- What have you heard about this initiative so far? → _Ask: Each identified stakeholder_
- What is your initial reaction — what excites you and what concerns you? → _Ask: Each identified stakeholder_
- On a scale of 1–10, how supportive are you of this change? What would move you higher? → _Ask: Each identified stakeholder_

**Influence:**
- When you express a view about a change, who listens? → _Ask: Each identified stakeholder_
- Who do your people go to for advice when they're uncertain about something new? → _Ask: Each identified stakeholder / Ask: Their direct reports_
- Are there informal leaders in your area whose opinion carries weight? Who? → _Ask: Each identified stakeholder_

**Needs:**
- What would you need from us to feel confident supporting this change? → _Ask: Each identified stakeholder_
- How do you prefer to be kept informed — and how often? → _Ask: Each identified stakeholder_

#### Answers
_(To be filled in per project)_

#### Particularisation Guidance
For **technology rollouts**, ensure you identify the IT stakeholders who control deployment, licensing, and security configuration — they can block adoption regardless of business support. For **process changes**, map the entire value chain to identify upstream and downstream stakeholders who may not be obvious. For **mergers and restructuring**, include stakeholders from both merging entities, and pay special attention to people who may lose power or status. Add questions about political dynamics and territory if the change crosses organisational boundaries.

### 3. Impact Analysis at the Role Level

#### To-dos
- [ ] Identify all distinct roles affected by the change (not just job titles — functional roles)
- [ ] For each role, document the current-state workflow (how they do their work today)
- [ ] For each role, document the future-state workflow (how they will do their work after the change)
- [ ] Map the gap between current and future state for each role: what disappears, what stays, what is new, what changes
- [ ] Create a structured impact analysis matrix: Role × Impact dimension (tools, processes, skills, reporting lines, performance metrics, workload)
- [ ] Rate the severity of impact per role (high/medium/low) to prioritise intervention effort
- [ ] Validate the impact analysis with middle managers and subject matter experts
- [ ] _[Project-specific: Add role-specific workflows and impact dimensions unique to the change being introduced]_

#### Questions

**Current State:**
- Walk me through a typical day/week in your role — what tools do you use and what tasks do you perform? → _Ask: End Users / Frontline Staff (sample per role)_
- Which parts of your current workflow are most time-consuming or frustrating? → _Ask: End Users / Frontline Staff_
- What workarounds have you developed to get things done? → _Ask: End Users / Frontline Staff_

**Future State Understanding:**
- What have you been told about how your work will change? → _Ask: End Users / Frontline Staff_
- What aspects of the change are unclear to you? → _Ask: End Users / Frontline Staff_

**Impact Assessment (ask managers):**
- Which of your team's workflows will be most affected by this change? → _Ask: Middle Managers_
- Will any tasks or responsibilities disappear entirely? Which ones? → _Ask: Middle Managers / IT Lead_
- What new tasks or skills will your team need to acquire? → _Ask: Middle Managers_
- Will performance metrics or KPIs change as a result? → _Ask: Middle Managers / Programme Manager_
- Will reporting lines or team structures change? → _Ask: Middle Managers / Senior Leadership_

**Capacity:**
- Does your team have capacity to learn new ways of working alongside their current workload? → _Ask: Middle Managers_
- What time of year or business cycle would make this change hardest to absorb? → _Ask: Middle Managers_

#### Answers
_(To be filled in per project)_

#### Particularisation Guidance
For **technology rollouts** (e.g., M365 Copilot), map impact per application per role — e.g., Finance uses Excel heavily, Communications uses Word and Outlook. For **process changes**, walk the end-to-end process with process owners and identify every handoff point that changes. For **restructuring**, the impact analysis must include emotional and identity impacts (loss of team, loss of role, change of manager), not just workflow changes. Add questions about seasonal business cycles if the change has timing implications.

### 4. Assess Current State Capability Baseline

#### To-dos
- [ ] Define what capabilities (skills, knowledge, behaviours) are relevant to assess for this change
- [ ] Design or select an assessment instrument (survey, skills test, self-assessment, observation)
- [ ] Pilot the assessment with a small group to validate clarity and relevance
- [ ] Deploy the assessment across all affected populations
- [ ] Analyse results and segment by role, department, location, and experience level
- [ ] Identify capability clusters: those already near-ready, those needing moderate upskilling, those needing significant support
- [ ] Document the baseline as a quantified reference point for measuring progress post-deployment
- [ ] Share findings with the training architecture design (→ feeds into Activity 10)
- [ ] _[Project-specific: Define the specific skills and tools to assess based on what is being introduced]_

#### Questions

**Current Skills:**
- How would you rate your proficiency with the current tools/processes being replaced or augmented? → _Ask: End Users (via survey)_
- What formal training have you received on your current tools in the last 12 months? → _Ask: End Users (via survey) / HR / L&D_
- What do you find most difficult about your current tools or processes? → _Ask: End Users (via survey or focus group)_

**Learning Readiness:**
- How do you prefer to learn new skills — classroom, online, peer coaching, self-directed? → _Ask: End Users (via survey)_
- How confident are you in your ability to learn new technology or processes? → _Ask: End Users (via survey)_
- What has your experience been with previous training programmes — were they helpful? → _Ask: End Users (via focus group)_

**Organisational Capability:**
- Does the organisation have an existing learning management system (LMS) or training infrastructure? → _Ask: HR / L&D_
- What training resources (trainers, rooms, e-learning platforms) are available? → _Ask: HR / L&D_
- Are there existing digital champions or super-users who could be leveraged? → _Ask: HR / L&D / IT Lead_

#### Answers
_(To be filled in per project)_

#### Particularisation Guidance
For **technology rollouts**, assess current digital literacy levels — not just familiarity with the specific tool, but general comfort with technology. Include questions about the current tool equivalent (e.g., "How do you currently use Excel?" before introducing Copilot in Excel). For **process changes**, assess current process compliance — if people aren't following the current process, introducing a new one has different implications. For **restructuring**, assess leadership and change management capabilities of managers who will need to lead their teams through the transition.

### 5. Organisational Change History and Scar Tissue Assessment

#### To-dos
- [ ] Identify the major changes the organisation has undergone in the last 2–3 years
- [ ] Select interviewees: long-tenured staff, middle managers, HR, and anyone who was involved in previous change efforts
- [ ] Schedule 30–45 minute interviews (individual or small group)
- [ ] Prepare the interview question set (see Questions below)
- [ ] Conduct interviews and document themes, patterns, and emotional residue
- [ ] Create a change history timeline noting: what was promised, what was delivered, what was the experience
- [ ] Identify "scar tissue" — areas where previous failed or painful changes have created cynicism, distrust, or resistance patterns
- [ ] Document implications for the current initiative: what to avoid, what to address explicitly, what narratives to counter
- [ ] _[Project-specific: Research specific past changes that are relevant to the current initiative, e.g., previous technology rollouts if this is a tech change]_

#### Questions

**Change History:**
- What significant changes has this organisation gone through in the last 2–3 years? → _Ask: Middle Managers / Long-tenured staff_
- Which of those changes went well? What made them successful? → _Ask: Middle Managers / Long-tenured staff_
- Which changes were painful or failed? What went wrong? → _Ask: Middle Managers / Long-tenured staff_

**Trust and Credibility:**
- When leadership announces a change, what is the typical reaction on the ground? → _Ask: Middle Managers / Long-tenured staff_
- Has there been a change where promises were made but not kept? What happened? → _Ask: Middle Managers / Long-tenured staff_
- Do people generally trust that changes are in their interest, or is there scepticism? → _Ask: Middle Managers / Long-tenured staff / HR_

**Patterns:**
- How are changes typically communicated here — and is it effective? → _Ask: Middle Managers / Long-tenured staff_
- Is there a pattern of "change fatigue" — too many changes at once? → _Ask: Middle Managers / HR_
- Have previous changes been properly completed, or do people feel like changes were abandoned halfway? → _Ask: Middle Managers / Long-tenured staff_

**Relevance to Current Initiative:**
- Has the organisation attempted anything similar to this change before? What happened? → _Ask: Middle Managers / IT Lead / Programme Manager_
- Are there specific groups or departments where change is particularly difficult? Why? → _Ask: HR / Senior Leadership_

#### Answers
_(To be filled in per project)_

#### Particularisation Guidance
For **technology rollouts**, specifically ask about previous technology implementations — did the last system go-live go smoothly? Was training adequate? Were workarounds needed? For **process changes**, ask about previous process improvement initiatives (e.g., Lean, Six Sigma) — were they sustained or did old habits return? For **mergers and restructuring**, this assessment is critical: ask about previous rounds of redundancies, restructuring, or leadership changes and the emotional residue they left. The scar tissue from these events can dominate the response to the current change.

### 6. Assess Middle Management Readiness and Willingness

#### To-dos
- [ ] Identify all middle managers whose teams are affected by the change
- [ ] Schedule small group conversations (3–5 managers per group, 60 minutes) or individual interviews for sensitive contexts
- [ ] Prepare the conversation guide (see Questions below)
- [ ] Conduct conversations and document each manager's readiness (ability to support the change) and willingness (desire to support the change)
- [ ] Plot managers on a readiness × willingness matrix
- [ ] For managers who are willing but not ready: plan capability building (training, coaching, resources)
- [ ] For managers who are ready but not willing: plan engagement and motivation interventions (address concerns, connect to their interests)
- [ ] For managers who are neither ready nor willing: escalate to sponsor for direct intervention
- [ ] Develop targeted support plans for each manager segment
- [ ] _[Project-specific: Add questions specific to the managerial capabilities this change requires, e.g., coaching digital skills if it's a tech rollout]_

#### Questions

**Understanding:**
- What do you understand about this change and why it's happening? → _Ask: Middle Managers_
- What questions do you have that haven't been answered? → _Ask: Middle Managers_
- Do you feel you have enough information to explain this change to your team? → _Ask: Middle Managers_

**Willingness:**
- How do you feel about this change personally — is it something you believe in? → _Ask: Middle Managers_
- What concerns do you have about the impact on your team? → _Ask: Middle Managers_
- Are you willing to actively advocate for this change with your team, even when it's difficult? → _Ask: Middle Managers_
- What would make you more confident or enthusiastic about supporting this? → _Ask: Middle Managers_

**Readiness:**
- Have you led your team through a significant change before? How did it go? → _Ask: Middle Managers_
- Do you feel equipped to support your team through this transition — training, coaching, handling resistance? → _Ask: Middle Managers_
- What support or resources would you need from us to lead this effectively? → _Ask: Middle Managers_

**Practical Concerns:**
- Do you and your team have capacity to take on this change alongside current workload? → _Ask: Middle Managers_
- What competing priorities might get in the way? → _Ask: Middle Managers_
- How will you know if your team is struggling with the change? → _Ask: Middle Managers_

#### Answers
_(To be filled in per project)_

#### Particularisation Guidance
For **technology rollouts**, assess managers' own digital competence — a manager who doesn't use the tool cannot credibly coach their team. Add questions like "Will you use this tool yourself?" and "How comfortable are you with technology?" For **process changes**, probe whether the manager was involved in designing the new process — involvement increases ownership. For **restructuring**, this conversation is extremely sensitive: managers may themselves be affected. Assess whether they know their own future before asking them to support their team's transition. Adjust the tone and framing accordingly.

### 7. Create Resistance Risk Profile

_Depends on: Activity 1 (Sponsorship), Activity 2 (Stakeholder Map), Activity 3 (Impact Analysis), Activity 5 (Change History), Activity 6 (Middle Management Assessment)_

#### To-dos
- [ ] Compile findings from Activities 1–6 into a single working document
- [ ] Identify all distinct affected populations (by role, department, location, or other meaningful segmentation)
- [ ] For each population, analyse what they stand to lose: status, competence, autonomy, workload predictability
- [ ] For each population, analyse what they might gain and whether they perceive it as a gain
- [ ] Assess structural factors for each population: quality of manager support, sponsor visibility, communication trust, prior change experience
- [ ] Rate resistance risk for each population (high/medium/low) with supporting evidence
- [ ] Document the resistance risk profile as a formal artefact
- [ ] Review and validate with the sponsor and programme manager
- [ ] _[Project-specific: Identify population-specific resistance factors unique to this change, e.g., for AI tools — fear of job replacement]_

#### Questions

**Loss Analysis (per population):**
- What status or recognition might this group lose? (e.g., being the expert, having a unique skill) → _Ask: Middle Managers / synthesise from Activity 3_
- What competence will they lose — will they go from expert to novice? → _Ask: Middle Managers / synthesise from Activity 4_
- What autonomy or control over their work might they lose? → _Ask: Middle Managers / synthesise from Activity 3_
- Will their workload increase during or after the transition? → _Ask: Middle Managers / Programme Manager_
- Will the predictability of their work decrease? → _Ask: Middle Managers / synthesise from Activity 3_

**Gain Analysis (per population):**
- What tangible benefits will this group experience? → _Ask: Programme Manager / Sponsor_
- Do they believe these benefits are real? Have they been communicated? → _Ask: End Users (via focus group)_
- When will they start experiencing the benefits — immediately or after a difficult transition period? → _Ask: Programme Manager / IT Lead_

**Structural Factors:**
- How supportive is this group's direct manager of the change? → _Synthesise from Activity 6_
- Has this group experienced broken promises from previous changes? → _Synthesise from Activity 5_
- Does this group trust organisational communications? → _Synthesise from Activity 5_

#### Answers
_(To be filled in per project)_

#### Particularisation Guidance
For **technology rollouts** (especially AI/automation), explicitly assess fear of job displacement — this is often the dominant resistance factor even if unstated. Add questions about whether roles will be eliminated. For **process changes**, the primary resistance factor is often workload — new processes almost always require more effort initially. For **restructuring**, loss of status and autonomy dominate. Tailor the loss analysis dimensions to match the type of change.

### 8. Create a Change Strategy Document

_Depends on: All Activities 1–7_

#### To-dos
- [ ] Draft the change strategy document structure: organisation context, change difficulty analysis, intervention plan, success criteria
- [ ] Write the organisational context section: who is this organisation, what are its characteristics, how does it handle change?
- [ ] Write the change difficulty analysis: what specifically makes this change hard for this organisation?
- [ ] Define named populations with specific intervention plans for each (drawing from the resistance risk profile)
- [ ] Define success criteria: what does successful adoption look like? At what levels? By when?
- [ ] Define the measurement approach (high level — detailed measurement in Activity 13)
- [ ] Include governance and escalation approach
- [ ] Review with sponsor and obtain sign-off
- [ ] _[Project-specific: Tailor the strategy to the specific change type and organisational context]_

#### Questions

**Strategy Framing:**
- What is the compelling case for this change? Why now? → _Synthesise from Activity 1_
- What are the top three risks to successful adoption? → _Synthesise from Activities 5, 6, 7_
- What is the single biggest obstacle we need to overcome? → _Ask: Sponsor / Programme Manager_

**Success Criteria:**
- How will we know this change has been successfully adopted? → _Ask: Sponsor / Senior Leadership_
- What adoption metrics matter most — utilisation, proficiency, or business impact? → _Ask: Sponsor / Programme Manager_
- What is the timeline for achieving full adoption? → _Ask: Sponsor / Programme Manager_
- What level of adoption is the minimum acceptable? → _Ask: Sponsor_

**Interventions:**
- For each population at high resistance risk: what specific intervention will address their concerns? → _Consultant synthesis from Activities 1–7_
- What resources are available for interventions? → _Ask: Programme Manager / Sponsor_
- Are there organisational constraints on the types of interventions we can use? (e.g., no mandatory training, no town halls, unionised environments) → _Ask: HR / Programme Manager_

#### Answers
_(To be filled in per project)_

#### Particularisation Guidance
For **technology rollouts**, the strategy should address the deployment sequence (who gets it first) and its change management implications. For **process changes**, focus on embedding the change into standard operating procedures so it's structurally reinforced. For **mergers**, the strategy must address cultural integration — not just process alignment. The strategy document format stays the same, but the weight given to each section shifts with the change type.

### 9. Design a Stakeholder Engagement Plan

_Depends on: Activity 2 (Stakeholder Map), Activity 7 (Resistance Risk Profile)_

#### To-dos
- [ ] Review the stakeholder map (from Activity 2) and resistance risk profile (from Activity 7)
- [ ] Prioritise stakeholders by influence and disposition: identify the "must-engage" list
- [ ] For each priority stakeholder, define the engagement approach: one-on-one, small group, or inclusion in a working group
- [ ] Build a coalition of supportive stakeholders: equip them with talking points, early access, and information advantage
- [ ] Plan early engagement with high-influence resistors — engage before the change becomes visible
- [ ] Create an engagement cadence: who to meet, when, what's needed at each stage, what they need from you
- [ ] Prepare talking points and briefing materials for supportive leaders to use with their teams
- [ ] Schedule all engagements and set up calendar invitations
- [ ] _[Project-specific: Identify stakeholder-specific engagement needs, e.g., a union engagement process, regulatory briefings, vendor coordination meetings]_

#### Questions

**Engagement Design:**
- For each key stakeholder: what do they need to know, feel, and do at each project stage? → _Consultant synthesis from Activities 1, 2, 7_
- What is the right frequency of engagement for each stakeholder? → _Ask: Programme Manager / Consultant judgement_
- Are there stakeholders who need to be engaged together (because they influence each other) or separately (because of political dynamics)? → _Ask: Sponsor / Programme Manager_

**Coalition Building:**
- Which supportive stakeholders have the most credibility with resistant groups? → _Synthesise from Activity 2_
- What do supportive stakeholders need from us to advocate effectively? → _Ask: Supportive Stakeholders_
- Are supportive stakeholders willing to speak publicly at town halls or in communications? → _Ask: Supportive Stakeholders_

**Resistance Engagement:**
- For each high-influence resistor: what is the root cause of their resistance? → _Synthesise from Activity 7_
- Is their resistance based on legitimate concerns that need addressing, or on personal interests? → _Consultant assessment_
- Who is the best person to engage each resistor — the sponsor, a peer, or the ACM consultant? → _Discuss with: Sponsor_

#### Answers
_(To be filled in per project)_

#### Particularisation Guidance
For **technology rollouts**, include IT leadership and security/compliance stakeholders in the engagement plan — they often control access and configuration. For **process changes**, engage process owners and quality teams who will need to update documentation. For **mergers**, the engagement plan must address stakeholders from both entities and be sensitive to power dynamics. Add engagement with works councils or unions if applicable to the jurisdiction.

### 10. Design the Training Architecture

_Depends on: Activity 3 (Impact Analysis), Activity 4 (Capability Baseline)_

#### To-dos
- [ ] Map the gap between current capability (Activity 4) and required future-state capability (Activity 3) per role
- [ ] Determine the training modality for each population: instructor-led, e-learning, peer coaching, self-paced, blended
- [ ] Decide training timing: before deployment, at deployment, or phased after deployment
- [ ] Define prerequisites: what must people know or have access to before training begins?
- [ ] Map training touchpoints in the deployment timeline with dependencies
- [ ] Identify who will deliver training: external trainers, internal L&D, champions, managers
- [ ] Estimate training duration and scheduling constraints per population
- [ ] Confirm training environment requirements (sandbox, test tenant, demo data)
- [ ] Document the training architecture (structure and approach — not content yet)
- [ ] _[Project-specific: Design role-specific training pathways based on the specific tool, process, or change being introduced]_

#### Questions

**Design Decisions:**
- What training modalities does the organisation already use and trust? → _Ask: HR / L&D_
- Are there scheduling constraints — can people be taken off their work for training? For how long? → _Ask: Middle Managers / HR_
- Is there a training environment or sandbox available for hands-on practice? → _Ask: IT Lead_
- Can training be recorded for later on-demand access? → _Ask: HR / L&D / IT Lead_

**Population Specifics:**
- For each affected role: what are the critical skills they must have from day one vs. what can be learned over time? → _Ask: Middle Managers / Programme Manager_
- Are there accessibility requirements (language, disability, remote workers)? → _Ask: HR / Middle Managers_
- Are there populations who will need significantly more support than others? → _Synthesise from Activity 4_

**Logistics:**
- How many people need to be trained, and over what timeframe? → _Ask: Programme Manager_
- Can training be delivered in the existing meeting/training infrastructure, or do we need additional facilities? → _Ask: HR / L&D / Facilities_
- What is the budget for training materials and delivery? → _Ask: Programme Manager / Sponsor_

#### Answers
_(To be filled in per project)_

#### Particularisation Guidance
For **technology rollouts**, design role-specific training paths (e.g., Finance team on Copilot in Excel, Communications on Word and Outlook). Include a safe practice environment so people can experiment without fear of breaking things. For **process changes**, training often needs to include "unlearning" — explicitly addressing why the old way is changing and what to stop doing. For **restructuring**, training may include leadership and change management skills for managers, not just operational skills.

### 11. Design Communication with Message Architecture

_Depends on: Activity 8 (Change Strategy), Activity 9 (Stakeholder Engagement Plan)_

#### To-dos
- [ ] Define the communication audiences (may differ from stakeholder groups — broader reach)
- [ ] Design the message sequence aligned to the audience's psychological journey:
  - Message 1: **Why?** — Why this change? Why now? Why us?
  - Message 2: **What?** — What will change specifically for me?
  - Message 3: **How?** — How to use the new thing (practical guidance)
- [ ] Define the communication channels for each audience (email, Teams, town hall, intranet, manager cascade, video)
- [ ] Ensure managers hear before their teams — build in a management pre-brief at each stage
- [ ] Create a communication timeline mapped to the deployment schedule
- [ ] Draft key messages and have them reviewed by the sponsor and programme manager
- [ ] Plan for two-way communication: how will questions and feedback be captured and responded to?
- [ ] _[Project-specific: Adapt messaging to address the specific "What's in it for me?" for each population and the specific change being introduced]_

#### Questions

**Audience and Channels:**
- What communication channels are most effective in this organisation? → _Ask: HR / Internal Communications / Middle Managers_
- Are there populations who don't regularly check email or intranet? How do we reach them? → _Ask: Middle Managers / HR_
- Is there an internal communications team we should coordinate with? → _Ask: Programme Manager / HR_
- Does the organisation have existing communication templates or brand guidelines we must follow? → _Ask: Internal Communications_

**Message Design:**
- What is the core "Why?" — the compelling reason for this change that will resonate with staff? → _Synthesise from Activity 1 / Ask: Sponsor_
- What are the top three concerns or questions staff are likely to have? → _Synthesise from Activities 5, 6, 7_
- What is the "What's in it for me?" for each audience? → _Synthesise from Activity 3 / Ask: Programme Manager_
- Are there messages from previous changes that need to be explicitly countered or addressed? → _Synthesise from Activity 5_

**Timing and Cascade:**
- How far in advance of deployment should the first communication go out? → _Ask: Programme Manager / Consultant judgement_
- Is there a management cascade process we can use — or do we need to create one? → _Ask: HR / Middle Managers_
- How will we handle rumours or misinformation? → _Ask: Sponsor / Programme Manager_

#### Answers
_(To be filled in per project)_

#### Particularisation Guidance
For **technology rollouts**, the "What changes for me?" message must be very specific and practical — avoid generic "this exciting new tool" language. Show before/after examples of actual workflows. For **process changes**, emphasise the rationale for the change and acknowledge what was good about the old way. For **mergers and restructuring**, communication is the most critical intervention — address job security concerns head-on and early, and avoid corporate euphemisms.

### 12. Get Technical Deployment Timeline and Dependencies

#### To-dos
- [ ] Obtain the full technical deployment timeline from the IT/technical project manager
- [ ] Map every technical milestone and identify its downstream ACM implications (e.g., "tenant configured" → "training environments available")
- [ ] Confirm pilot environment availability dates and scope
- [ ] Confirm go-live date and any hard dependencies (e.g., licence activation, security approvals)
- [ ] Identify the rollback plan: what happens if go-live fails?
- [ ] Build the ACM plan against the technical timeline — align all communication, training, and support activities to technical milestones
- [ ] Identify risks: what technical delays would cascade into ACM plan disruption?
- [ ] Establish a regular sync cadence with the technical team
- [ ] _[Project-specific: Map technology-specific deployment steps and their ACM implications]_

#### Questions

**Timeline:**
- What is the confirmed deployment timeline with key milestones? → _Ask: IT Lead / Programme Manager_
- Which milestones are firm vs. tentative? → _Ask: IT Lead / Programme Manager_
- What are the dependencies between technical milestones? → _Ask: IT Lead_
